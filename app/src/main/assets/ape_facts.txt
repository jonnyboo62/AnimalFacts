Apes are primates, a group that includes monkeys, humans, and their relatives.
Unlike monkeys, apes do not have tails.
The main groups of apes are great apes and lesser apes.
Great apes include gorillas, orangutans, chimpanzees, bonobos, and humans.
Lesser apes mainly refer to gibbons and siamangs.
Apes primarily live in the tropical forests of Africa and Asia.
Chimpanzees use tools, such as sticks to fish for termites, showing their problem-solving skills.
Orangutans are known for their strong arms, used for swinging from tree to tree in the rainforest.
Gorillas live in groups called troops, led by a dominant male known as a silverback.
Bonobos are known for their peaceful and cooperative societies, often resolving conflicts through social bonds and behaviors.
Apes communicate using vocalizations, gestures, and facial expressions.
The diet of apes varies from fruits and leaves to, in some cases, small animals and insects.

Chimpanzees and bonobos are our closest living relatives, sharing over 98% of their DNA with humans.
Gorillas can be divided into two species: the Eastern Gorilla and the Western Gorilla, each with two subspecies.
Some apes, particularly chimpanzees and orangutans, have been observed making and using tools in the wild.
Apes have complex social structures and can show a wide range of emotions such as joy, sadness, and empathy.
Conservation status of many ape species is critical due to habitat destruction, poaching, and disease.
Gibbons are known for their acrobatic prowess and their ability to sing complex songs to communicate with others.
The intelligence of apes is demonstrated by their ability to learn sign language and use symbols to communicate with humans.
Apes have played significant roles in human culture and mythology across various societies.
The study of ape behavior and intelligence provides insights into human evolution and social behavior.
Apes possess the ability for cultural transmission, passing down knowledge and skills to successive generations.
Genomic studies have shown that humans and chimpanzees have a common ancestor, dating back approximately 6 to 7 million years ago.
Apes have been observed to exhibit mourning behaviors, indicating a perception of death and loss.
The Yerkes-Dodson law, originating from studies on apes, describes how an optimal level of arousal enhances learning and performance.
Apes have a varied microbiome that is essential for their digestive health, similar to humans.
Tool use among apes is not just limited to foraging; it also includes social interactions and altering their environment.
Cognitive experiments with apes have demonstrated their ability to plan for future events, a trait once considered uniquely human.
Advanced genomic techniques have enabled scientists to explore the evolution of language and cognition by comparing human and ape genomes.
